## How to approach the paid-consult conversation

- Frame it plainly: *"I'm not looking for a bid yet. I'd like to pay for two hours of your time to walk my site, answer some technical questions, and help me think through design and feasibility before I commit. What do you charge for that?"*
- Typical rate for a professional consultation in Connecticut: **$150–$350 for a 90-to-120-minute site visit**, depending on the contractor's experience level and whether they produce a written summary or sketch. Some deck specialists will apply the consult fee toward the project if you hire them; negotiate that upfront.
- Some contractors will resist a paid consult and push toward a free bid. A confident specialist will respect the ask. If they can't quote a rate for their time, that tells you something.
- Bring your property survey/plat to the site visit. Know your setback lines. The contractor should be asking about those — if they don't, that's a flag.

## Questions Thomas should ask on the consult

1. **Frost line:** *"What footing depth do you specify for this climate, and why?"* The correct answer for Connecticut is 42 inches minimum. Anything less is a code problem and a structural one.

2. **Ledger attachment:** *"How do you attach the ledger to the house, and what flashing system do you use?"* Ledger failure from water infiltration is the single most common cause of deck collapse. A specialist should have a detailed answer without hesitating.

3. **Permits:** *"You pull the permit — not me, right?"* Correct answer: yes. If they suggest the homeowner pull the permit, end the conversation. That shifts liability to you.

4. **Composite vs. pressure-treated tradeoffs:** *"Given CT winters and my site conditions, what would you build if it were your house?"* You want an opinionated answer backed by reasoning, not a product brochure.

5. **Subcontractors:** *"Is your crew your employees, or are they subs? If subs, do they carry their own workers' comp?"* Workers' comp on subcontractors is your liability exposure if they're injured on your property.

6. **Warranty and callback:** *"What's your warranty, and have you ever had to honor it? Tell me about one."* A contractor who claims zero callbacks is either lying or hasn't been in business long enough.

7. **Timeline and sequencing:** *"When would you realistically start, and what's the typical build time for a project of this scope?"* Hartford-area building departments vary in permit turnaround — ask whether they factor that into their schedule.

## Red flags to watch for during the consult

- **No COI on request.** If they can't produce a current Certificate of Insurance naming you as certificate holder within 24 hours of you asking, stop. Every legitimate contractor has this ready.
- **Down payment above one-third.** Connecticut law caps home improvement down payments at 1/3 of the contract value. Anyone asking for 50% or "half up front to secure materials" is either ignorant of CT law or exploiting you — neither is acceptable.
- **Cash only or no written contract.** A written contract is legally required for any job over $200 in Connecticut. It must include HIC registration number, start and substantial completion dates, and a notice of the CT Home Improvement Guaranty Fund.
- **"I need you to pull the permit."** This is a liability trap, not a time-saver. If a contractor asks the homeowner to pull the permit, they are telling you they either can't or won't stand behind the work in the eyes of the building department.
- **Pressure to sign same day.** Any "this price expires tonight" language is a manipulation tactic. A confident contractor with a full schedule doesn't need it.
- **No local references from the last 12 months.** Ask for three addresses of completed projects. Drive past them. Call the owners. If they can't produce current references, their active roster may not exist.

## Next steps

- **Verify HIC registrations** for every finalist before the consult, not after. Go to elicense.ct.gov, search by company name and by license number. Confirm status is "Active" — not expired, not suspended.
- **Request a Certificate of Insurance** naming you (Thomas, at your address) as the certificate holder. The COI must show general liability AND workers' compensation separately, with policy numbers and expiration dates. A COI showing only GL with no WC on a contractor with a crew is incomplete.
- **Get three written bids on identical scope** — same materials, same square footage, same railing spec, same permit language — before committing to anyone. Two bids is a coin flip. Three lets the middle bid anchor the range and surfaces outliers on both ends.
