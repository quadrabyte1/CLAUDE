## Process Memo

### Why Helical Piers Are Worth Considering for a Deck in Hartford

Hartford sits on a mix of clay-rich glacial till and fill soils — common throughout the Connecticut River valley — that can shift, heave, and settle when freeze-thaw cycles hit an inadequately founded structure. The state's frost depth is 42 inches (required minimum per the 2022 Connecticut State Building Code). A concrete tube footing dug to 42 inches takes time, cold weather delays, and cure time before framing can begin. A helical pier screws into the ground past frost in a matter of minutes per pier, produces a torque log that demonstrates load capacity in real time, and is ready to frame on the same day. There is no concrete to cure, no waiting for inspections of wet forms, and no spoil pile in your yard. In difficult or waterlogged soils — common in Hartford's urban core — helical piers often reach competent bearing soil more reliably than a hand-dug tube footing. Additionally, if you ever need to relocate or remove the deck, the piers can be extracted; concrete cannot.

### The Hartford Permit Situation

**A building permit is required for residential decks in Hartford** whenever the deck: (a) is attached to the house, (b) exceeds 200 square feet, (c) rises more than 30 inches above grade, or (d) serves a required exit door. Most residential decks meet at least one trigger. The 2022 Connecticut State Building Code (effective October 1, 2022) governs permit applications. Frost depth: 42 inches minimum, measured to the bottom of the footing.

For helical piers specifically: CT State Building Inspector Interpretation **I-24-03** (issued August 2, 2024) clarifies that when certified helical piers are used for single- and two-family residential deck footings, they are treated as **alternative materials and methods under the IRC** and do not require a licensed engineer's stamp — provided the contractor supplies the manufacturer's ICC-ES or IAPMO evaluation report and documentation that the piers meet the load requirements of R301. In practice, this means the pier installer submits cut sheets and the torque log to the building department; no PE stamp needed for a standard residential deck. However: **if your deck has unusual loads** (hot tub, roof, heavy planters) **or if the Hartford building department has questions about the layout**, they may request an engineer's review. Ask your installer whether they have a structural engineer on call for this.

**The contractor pulls the permit — never the homeowner.** This is not optional; it is the legally correct division of liability. A contractor who asks you to pull the permit is either not registered with Connecticut DCP or is trying to shift liability onto you. This is a hard stop.

### Recommended Next Steps

Request **three written bids on identical scope** from the finalists below. Bids are not comparable unless they are scoped identically. Your identical-scope request should specify:

- Whether an engineered pier layout will be provided, or just manufacturer spec sheets
- Pier diameter and blade configuration
- Warranty term and what it covers
- Whether a torque log per pier is included in the deliverable (it must be — this is your proof of installation quality and your permit document)

### Red Flags Specific to This Trade

- **"We drive steel pipe with a hammer and call it helical."** No. True helical piers screw into the ground using a hydraulic drive head with a torque transducer. If there is no torque reading, there is no capacity verification.
- **Cash-only.** No. This is a permit-required trade. A cash-only installer is not pulling a permit.
- **No torque log / installation report.** No. Every pier in a real helical installation gets its own torque log entry. This document is your warranty claim basis and your permit closeout document. Walk away from any installer who cannot describe what a torque log is.
- **"You pull the permit."** No. Hard stop. The contractor pulls the permit in their name.
- **No engineer involvement when the building department asks for it.** No. Some Hartford reviewers will ask questions. A qualified installer has a relationship with a structural engineer who can stamp a layout if needed.
- **No ICC-ES or IAPMO evaluation report.** No. Code compliance hinges on this document. Ask for the report number before you agree to anything.
