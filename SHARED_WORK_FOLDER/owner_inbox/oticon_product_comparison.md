## How to read this table

**Chip platform — what it actually drives.**
- **Sirius (2024+):** 2nd-gen DNN, on-aid OTA updates, BT LE Audio + Auracast, 4D Sensor in supporting families. This is "current generation."
- **Polaris R (2023):** Polaris with hardened processing for sudden/wind/handling sounds. Drives Real only.
- **Polaris (2020):** First DNN-trained chip; still in active use across Own, Zircon, Play PX, CROS PX, Jet PX. Capable, just one generation behind Sirius.
- **Velox S (2019):** Pre-DNN. Lives on in **Xceed** (because the power-aid market hasn't moved chips) and in **Ruby** (because the budget tier is built around it). Solid sound, but no neural-net processing.

**Performance tiers (1 / 2 / 3 / 4).**
Within most families, Oticon sells the same hardware at multiple feature tiers. Lower numbers = more features unlocked.
- **1 = Premium:** all features on, max environments, max channels, max adaptive personalization.
- **2 = Advanced:** most premium features, slightly fewer adaptive environments.
- **3 = Standard:** core feature set, fewer environments.
- **4 = Essential** *(Intent only):* entry-level Intent; core sound but no 4D Sensor.
4D Sensor (Intent's marquee feature) is **tier 1 & 2 only**. Going to tier 3/4 saves money but gives up Intent's distinguishing feature — at that point, Real often wins on value.

**Form-factor codes.**
miniRITE = receiver-in-the-ear (most common modern style). **R** = rechargeable Li-ion; **T** = disposable size-312 zinc-air. miniBTE = behind-the-ear with thin tube. BTE PP / BTE UP = behind-the-ear Power Plus / Ultra Power for severe-profound. ITE/ITC/CIC/IIC = custom in-the-ear shells, descending in size and visibility.

**Headline differences at a glance.**
- **Flagship today:** Intent (Sirius + 4D Sensor).
- **Most discreet *with* connectivity:** Zeal (Sirius CIC).
- **Most discreet, period:** Own SI IIC (no radio, no app — just tiny).
- **Most powerful:** Xceed (Velox S, profound loss).
- **Pediatric first-fit:** Play SI; Play PX still fitted on the older platform.
- **Best value-with-DNN:** Jet PX. Best value-without-DNN: Zircon, Ruby.
- **Disposable-battery flagship:** Verit (the only Sirius family without a rechargeable variant).
- **Single-sided deafness:** CROS PX, paired with any compatible Oticon RITE.

**Spec gaps / caveats.**
- Verit's exact tier count (1/2/3 vs. 1/2) and Zeal's tier mapping are not consistently published across third-party sites — listed values are best-confirmed estimates.
- Ruby is still listed by some retailers in 2026 but is missing from Oticon's headline 2026 lineup; its long-term fate is unclear and it may quietly age out as Jet PX expands.
- More is functionally legacy: not Oticon's first-fit recommendation, but firmware/service still active and millions of units in field.

## Pricing notes

**MSRP isn't a thing in this industry.** Oticon — like every major hearing-aid manufacturer (Phonak, Signia, Widex, ReSound, Starkey) — does **not** publish a manufacturer's suggested retail price for consumer hearing aids. The product is sold almost exclusively through licensed audiologists and dispensers as a **bundled professional service**, where the price the patient pays covers:

- the device itself,
- the hearing test and fitting appointment,
- programming and verification (REM),
- typically 2–3 years of follow-up visits and remote-care support,
- manufacturer warranty (3 years on most current Oticon models),
- loss-and-damage coverage,
- and often consumables (domes, wax filters, sometimes batteries).

Two patients buying the same Intent 1 from two different clinics can legitimately pay $2,000 apart based on the bundle, the region, and how much chair time the audiologist commits.

**Per pair vs. per device.** Hearing aids are quoted in pairs by industry convention because most patients have bilateral loss. CROS PX is the exception — it's one transmitter added to a single hearing aid on the better ear, and is priced per device. All other ranges in the table are **per pair**.

**The performance-tier price ladder.** Within each family Oticon sells the same physical hardware at multiple feature tiers; lower numbers unlock more features. The tier ladder typically spans **~$1,500–$2,500 per pair** between the family's premium tier 1 and its essential tier (3 or 4). For example, on Intent the published range runs roughly $2,900 (tier 4) → $7,600 (tier 1) across the network of US clinics.

**Two-channel pricing reality.**
- **Online / network channels** (ZipHearing, Soundly, HearUp, certain warehouse-club partners) post the lower end of each range — typically $2,400–$4,500 per pair — and bundle a thinner service package (often 1 year of follow-up vs. 3).
- **Independent local clinics** sit at the higher end ($4,500–$7,600 per pair at tier 1) with the full 3-year service bundle. This is not gouging; it's a different product.

**Pediatric and CROS are quoted differently.**
- **Play SI / Play PX** are rarely cash-pay. State Early Hearing Detection and Intervention (EHDI) programs, school-district funding, and most private insurance plans cover pediatric hearing aids fully or partially, so retailers don't typically post prices the way they do adult lines.
- **CROS PX** is a single-device add-on and is priced per device, not per pair. Total bilateral cost = (one CROS PX transmitter) + (one host hearing aid on the hearing ear).

**Newly-released families have thin pricing data.** Verit launched April 2026 and Zeal launched January 2026; HearingTracker and ZipHearing are still aggregating dispenser quotes. Newly-released Oticon aids historically track the prior-generation flagship pricing within ±10%, which is the basis for the Verit estimate above.

**What the owner is actually paying for.** A useful mental model: of a $6,000-per-pair clinic-bundled tier 1 quote, roughly $1,800–$2,400 is the hardware (manufacturer wholesale + clinic margin), and the rest is the service envelope. Unbundled pricing (device-only, "I'll find my own audiologist") is technically available but rare in the US — Costco's Kirkland Signature is the dominant unbundled-style channel, and Costco does not currently carry Oticon (Kirkland Signature 11.0 is Rexton/Sonova-made).
