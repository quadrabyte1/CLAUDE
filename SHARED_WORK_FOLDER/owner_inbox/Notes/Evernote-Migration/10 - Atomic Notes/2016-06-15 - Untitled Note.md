Inverse v. Converse

Wednesday, June 15, 2016

10:31 AM

I was just reading about the Millennium Prizes in Mathematics (diverted here from a legal theory paper about the N v. NP problem in computer science — really interesting), and I came across a prime example of confusion between converse and inverse: 

 

Notwithstanding the wording of the problem descriptions, the SAB will not consider recommending the award of a prize to any individual who has not, in the judgement of SAB, made a major personal contribution to the understanding of the field of the problem in their published solution; nor will it investigate in detail solutions that do not represent a major advance in the field. ***Conversely, the SAB may consider recommending the award of the prize to an individual who has published work that, in the judgement of the SAB, fully resolves the questions raised by one of the Millennium Prize Problems even if it does not exactly meet the wording in the official problem description.***

Here, I think they meant "inversely."  The original proposition was "if the solution conforms to the wording but not the substance \[P\], the prize will not be awarded \[Q\]," and the second proposition is "if the solution conforms to the substance but not the wording \[not-P\], the prize will be awarded \[not-Q\]."  (There's an added layer of complexity here because the consequent of the second proposition is probably better described as "the prize *may* be awarded," but the switch from "will not" to "may" (as opposed to "will not" to "will") is incidental, I think, to the logical point.  The true converse of the first proposition would have been something like: "if no prize is awarded notwithstanding the solution's conformity to the wording of the prize, it follows that the solution was judged by the committee to be insufficiently in keeping with the prize's substance."  Or something like that.
