## 2. Executive Summary

**Overall risk tier: YELLOW** (with meaningful RED-leaning mark-strength concerns)

**One-paragraph verdict.** No identical or highly similar **live federal** USPTO registration for "EVERY GOLFER'S DREAM" or the close variant "GOLFER'S DREAM" surfaced in the free public trademark indexes. Primary .com (everygolfersdream.com) is **NOT available** — it has been held by DirectNic since July 2000 with all modification locks set, with no live website; effectively a long-term squat or speculative hold. A Canadian golf course ("Golfer's Dream Golf Club," Port Perry Ontario, ~16+ years operating) uses the close variant as an active trade name in a related class (Class 41 / 43). A widely-known 2007 golf book by Larry Berle titled **"A Golfer's Dream"** (Amazon-sold, Minnesota Book Award nominee) establishes prior common-law use of the close variant in golf publishing. Most importantly, the phrase "every golfer's dream" appears as casual, descriptive, laudatory copy in hundreds of social posts, YouTube titles, PGA posts, golf blogs — which both (a) signals the mark is **borderline descriptive / laudatory** under Lanham Act §2(e)(1) and USPTO practice on laudatory terms, and (b) means the mark, if registered at all, would receive narrow scope and be hard to enforce against competitors.

**Top three concerns (bullets):**
- The phrase is **laudatory/descriptive** ("best-of-class praise") which the USPTO's own §2(e)(1) guidance lists alongside "best, greatest, supreme, preferred" as presumptively descriptive — likely Principal Register refusal absent acquired distinctiveness; Supplemental Register likely the realistic outcome.
- **everygolfersdream.com** is taken (2000 registration, DirectNic, all client locks), severely limiting digital brand build-out and likely requiring an expensive aftermarket acquisition or a compromise TLD.
- A Canadian golf course (**Golfer's Dream Golf Club / golfersdream.ca**) and a US-published book (**"A Golfer's Dream," Larry Berle, 2007, ISBN 9780615145754**) create common-law shadows in adjacent golf-services and golf-publishing space, which will weigh against Thomas in any DuPont factor-6 ("number and nature of similar marks on similar goods") analysis.

**Recommended next step:** Proceed only with (i) attorney review, (ii) a serious honest conversation about whether a laudatory name is right for a brand that will need real enforceable IP, and (iii) a parallel-track investigation of whether a stronger, more distinctive mark would be a better investment of Thomas's time and capital. If Thomas still wants this name after (i)–(iii), the attorney should explore Supplemental Register filing, a stylized/logo composite application, or a narrower classes/services description — and Thomas must budget for aftermarket acquisition of everygolfersdream.com or accept a weaker TLD position.

## 3. Mark Strength Analysis

**Distinctiveness spectrum placement:** **Suggestive — laudatory, borderline descriptive.**

- **Generic?** No — the phrase is not the generic name of any good/service category.
- **Descriptive?** **Likely yes under §2(e)(1)** for the intended goods. "Every Golfer's Dream" is structurally a laudatory/puffery phrase of the form "the ideal [purchaser]'s [aspiration]" — which tracks with USPTO guidance that laudatory terms ("best," "friendly," "majestic," "dependable," "preferred," "greatest," "deluxe," "supreme") are merely descriptive under §2(e)(1). The phrase describes an aspirational quality of the product (a commemorative plaque of your hole-in-one IS a dream-come-true for a golfer), which is precisely the use-case the USPTO flags as descriptive laudatory. Sources: [Alt Legal — Overcoming 2(e)(1)](https://www.altlegal.com/blog/a-distinct-category-how-to-overcome-a-2e1-refusal/), [TRAMATM Q&A on §2(e)(1)](https://www.tramatm.com/en/trademark-questions-and-answers/office-actions/what-is-section-2e1-refusal-mere-descriptiveness).
- **Suggestive?** Borderline. Under the "imagination test" (does the consumer need imagination to connect the mark to the product?), the connection between "every golfer's dream" and "commemorative 3D-printed plaque of your hole-in-one" is not immediate — it requires the consumer to understand the product category first — which is suggestive. But against apparel, golf instruction, golf travel, golf equipment, etc., the leap is minimal or nonexistent, which would make the mark descriptive in those classes.
- **Arbitrary / fanciful?** No.

**Registrability implications.**
- **Principal Register:** likely refused under §2(e)(1) as merely descriptive for the primary intended goods/services; at minimum, the examining attorney is likely to issue a §2(e)(1) office action requiring response. Overcoming requires (i) argument that the mark is suggestive (possible but an uphill fight given the evidence of widespread descriptive use — see §6), or (ii) a §2(f) acquired-distinctiveness showing (typically requires 5+ years of substantially exclusive and continuous use plus consumer-recognition evidence — Thomas does not have this).
- **Supplemental Register:** likely achievable — Supplemental is the fallback for descriptive-but-not-generic marks, and gives limited statutory rights (ability to use ®, TTAB jurisdiction, priority for later Principal filing once acquired distinctiveness develops).
- **Stylized/logo composite mark:** the examiner may still refuse based on the disclaimed word portion, but a distinctive logo paired with the words is a common path.

**Other §2 flags checked:**
- §2(a) disparagement/deceptiveness: no concern.
- §2(b) flag/insignia: no concern.
- §2(c) living person: no concern.
- §2(d) likelihood of confusion: addressed in §4, §6.
- §2(e)(2)/(3)/(4) geographically descriptive / primarily a surname: no concern.

**Plain-English verdict on mark strength:** Weak. The brand will be expensive to police, easy for a competitor to come close to without infringing, and hard to register on the Principal Register without a multi-year distinctiveness build.

## 12. Overall Risk Conclusion

**Overall risk tier: YELLOW.**

**Why not GREEN:** the mark is laudatory/borderline descriptive under §2(e)(1), the primary .com is held (2000-era squat), and there are meaningful common-law neighbors in golf (Canadian golf course, US book, crowded "Dream [Golf Noun]" field).

**Why not RED:** no identical live federal registration surfaced for any variant; no US common-law user is a direct blocker in the primary Class 20/21 plaque category; social handles are at least partially available (YouTube, LinkedIn confirmed); the mark is unlikely to attract a §2(d) opposition from a dominant senior user.

**Summary of mitigations available:**
1. **Attorney-led Supplemental Register filing** (accepts descriptive; builds distinctiveness history; §2(f) upgrade path to Principal in 5 years).
2. **Stylized/composite mark filing** — distinctive logo paired with the words, with the word portion potentially disclaimed.

4. **US-only territorial scope initially** — postpone Madrid extension into Canada until the Port Perry course situation is assessed by counsel.
5. **Acquire everygolfersdream.com** — budget a good-faith aftermarket offer; dormant 2000-era holds are often purchasable in the low-to-mid four figures, sometimes higher for niche phrase holds.

7. **Lock social handles immediately** — YouTube and LinkedIn look free; verify and claim Instagram, TikTok, X, Facebook, Threads, Pinterest manually before any public announcement.
8. **Seriously consider a more distinctive alternative name.** "Every Golfer's Dream" will not be a strong trademark asset; a suggestive or arbitrary mark would give Thomas a far stronger enforcement position for the same dollar of filing cost. This is the conversation worth having with an attorney or brand strategist.
