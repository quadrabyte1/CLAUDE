# Hiring Research: Brand & Trademark Clearance Specialist

**Prepared by:** Pax (Senior Researcher)
**Date:** 2026-04-23
**Trigger:** Larry is hiring a clearance specialist to vet proposed brand names across federal/state trademarks, common law, domains, social handles, marketplaces, and international registries, and to produce go/no-go risk reports.

---

## 1. Role Overview

Real-world practitioners in this space do not use one consistent title. The closest human archetypes:

- **Trademark Paralegal (Clearance focus)** — non-attorney who runs searches, compiles reports, and drafts clearance memos for an attorney's review and sign-off. Median US comp ~$75K–$110K, senior ~$149K. NALA's Advanced Paralegal Certification in IP Law is the typical credential.
- **Trademark Search Analyst / Clearance Researcher** — often works inside vendor shops like CompuMark, Corsearch, or Markify; specializes in producing the raw "full availability" search report that attorneys then analyze.
- **Brand Clearance Researcher / Naming Availability Analyst** — lives in branding agencies (Interbrand, Lexicon, Siegel+Gale, NameStormers). Partners with creative naming teams; runs availability sweeps on candidate names before they are presented to clients.
- **IP Paralegal — Clearance** — in-house at larger companies (AbbVie, P&G, Nike). Coordinates clearance for new product/style names, liaises with outside counsel.
- **Trademark Administrator (TMA)** — INTA-recognized role; broader portfolio lifecycle (clearance, prosecution, maintenance, enforcement), but clearance is a core piece.

For Larry's team, the best archetype is a **Brand & Trademark Clearance Specialist** — a non-attorney researcher who (a) runs federal, state, common-law, domain, social, and marketplace sweeps on a proposed name, (b) applies the DuPont likelihood-of-confusion framework to surface risk, and (c) delivers a structured clearance memo with a risk tier and explicit "not legal advice" disclaimer.

**Critical boundary:** the specialist is NOT a lawyer. They produce factual search reports and risk flags. A final "you may use/register this mark" opinion is the practice of law and must come from a licensed trademark attorney. The USPTO has tightened UPL (Unauthorized Practice of Law) rules in recent years; the specialist's deliverable must stay on the research-and-surface side of that line.

## 2. Core Skills

### Legal literacy (not licensure)
- **Lanham Act §2(d)** — likelihood-of-confusion bar to registration.
- **DuPont factors** (In re E. I. DuPont de Nemours & Co., 1973) — the 13-factor framework USPTO and TTAB use. The first two (mark similarity + goods/services similarity) carry most of the weight.
- **Spectrum of distinctiveness** — generic < descriptive < suggestive < arbitrary < fanciful. Knowing where a candidate mark falls predicts both registrability and scope of protection.
- **Nice Classification** — 45 classes (1–34 goods, 35–45 services); skilled at identifying overlap (e.g., sunglasses straddle Class 9 and Class 25; a restaurant that bottles sauce needs Class 43 and Class 30).
- **Live vs. dead marks** — dead marks aren't automatic green lights; residual common-law rights may survive.
- **Principal vs. Supplemental Register** — where a mark lives changes the conflict calculus.

### Search craft
- **Phonetic equivalents** — `[aeiou]` wildcards, double-letter variants, dropped vowels (e.g., GRYD vs. GRID, FONETIX vs. PHONETICS).
- **Truncation and root searching** — find family marks (e.g., MCDONALD's, MCCAFE, MCNUGGET share MC- root).
- **Translations and transliterations** — doctrine of foreign equivalents means a Spanish word may conflict with its English meaning.
- **Design code searching** — USPTO Design Search Code Manual; e.g., DC:050101 = triangular/conical trees. Required when a logo is in scope.
- **Boolean operators** — the new USPTO Trademark Search system (replaced TESS in Nov 2023) supports field tags, proximity, and wildcards; the specialist must be fluent in its query syntax.

### Research breadth
- Internet and social media sweeps (Google, Bing, Instagram, TikTok, X, YouTube, LinkedIn, Reddit).
- Industry-specific trade publications and directories.
- Domain TLD coverage and aftermarket.
- Marketplace storefront searches (Amazon Brand Registry conflicts, Etsy listings, Shopify stores, eBay).

### Report writing
- Structured clearance memos with consistent sections, severity tiers, and the correct disclaimers.
- Plain-language summaries for non-lawyer founders and marketers.
- Citation of exact serial/registration numbers, class numbers, and hit URLs so an attorney can verify.

## 3. Tools & Databases

### Federal (US) — free
- **USPTO Trademark Search System** (https://tmsearch.uspto.gov) — replaced TESS in Nov 2023. Live + dead marks, Principal + Supplemental Register. Supports phonetic queries via vowel wildcards and design-code lookups.
- **USPTO TSDR** — status and document history for any application or registration by serial/reg number.
- **USPTO Design Search Code Manual** — numerical codes for figurative elements (for logo/design searches).
- **USPTO ID Manual** — pre-approved descriptions of goods/services, maps to Nice classes.
- **TTAB (Trademark Trial and Appeal Board)** — for opposition and cancellation history on live marks.

### State — free
- **Every US Secretary of State** publishes a business entity and (usually) a state trademark database. Coverage and UX vary wildly. Good aggregators/indices: LLCStarters, Wolters Kluwer state guides.
- State databases are essential because (a) federal registration is optional, and (b) state business entity names can create confusion even without a state TM filing.

### International — free
- **WIPO Global Brand Database** (https://www.wipo.int/en/web/global-brand-database) — 50M+ records from 70+ databases, including Madrid System. Image similarity search included.
- **WIPO Madrid Monitor** — live status of international Madrid applications/registrations.
- **EUIPO TMview** — 130M+ marks across 54 offices including US, Canada, Mexico.
- **EUIPO eSearch plus** — EU marks specifically.
- **CIPO Canadian Trademarks Database** — Canadian marks 1865–present.
- **EUIPO TMclass** — Nice classification assistant.

### Paid / AI-powered (gated)
- **CompuMark** (Clarivate) — 100M+ records, 180+ jurisdictions; text, phonetic, linguistic, image, AI-visual similarity. Enterprise-priced; the traditional gold standard.
- **Corsearch** — AI screening + full clearance + watch services. ~$12,320/yr quoted for USPTO+state unlimited text searches. Now owns TrademarkNow.
- **TrademarkNow (Corsearch)** — ExaMatch (190+ registries), NameCheck (rapid candidate screening), LogoCheck (figurative marks). Claims 40% timeline reduction and 50–70% watch-review time reduction.
- **TM TKO (Anaqua)** — 70M+ records, 180+ jurisdictions; knockout + phonetic + visual similarity + analytics. ~$5K+/user/yr.
- **Markify** — freemium/subscription; limit of 5 classes per search standard; rated highest hit-rate (~99.7% in comparative tests) for phonetic/fuzzy matching. Cheaper entry point than CompuMark/Corsearch.
- **Trademarkia** — freemium; popular but often flagged as thin vs. the enterprise tools.
- **Alt Legal** — docketing + some search workflow tooling.

### Domain
- **Instant Domain Search, Domainr, Namecheap, Name.com** — live TLD availability across 800+ extensions.
- **WHOIS / RDAP** — who.is, whois.com, registrar RDAP endpoints for ownership + expiration.
- **Afternic, Sedo, Atom, Dan.com** — aftermarket/premium listing prices.
- **ICANN UDRP precedent (udrpsearch.com, WIPO UDRP decisions)** — for bad-faith/cybersquatting history on conflicting domains.

### Social handles
- **Namechk** — scans 100+ platforms and 36 TLDs with color-coded availability.
- **KnowEm** — 500+ websites, can link through to sign-up pages for reservation; paid tier auto-registers.
- **Namecheckly, Brandsnag, Handle Monitor, socialusernamecheck.com** — modern alternatives.
- Direct manual checks on Instagram, X/Twitter, TikTok, YouTube, Facebook, LinkedIn, Reddit, Pinterest, Threads, Bluesky — because aggregators occasionally lie (handle exists but is private/shadowbanned).

### Marketplace
- Amazon (incl. Brand Registry conflict check), Etsy search + shop directory, Shopify storefront search (harder — often requires Google site: queries), eBay stores, Walmart Marketplace, Mercari.

### Copyright / adjacent
- **copyright.gov Public Catalog** — for registered slogans, taglines, logo artwork (copyright, not trademark, but sometimes catches overlapping creative claims).

## 4. Typical Workflow

The industry-standard sequence is a two-stage funnel: **Knockout** (fast, cheap, obvious kills) → **Full/Comprehensive** (slow, thorough, decision-ready).

### Stage 0 — Intake
- Capture the proposed mark(s), goods/services description in plain English, target geography (US-only, US+Canada, Madrid-global), and whether a logo is in scope.
- Map the goods/services to Nice classes (typically 1–3 classes for most small-business brands).
- Classify the mark on the distinctiveness spectrum — flag generic/descriptive candidates before spending search budget.

### Stage 1 — Knockout (1–2 business days)
- USPTO exact-match and close-variant search across relevant classes + related classes.
- Google + Bing top-page sweep for obvious commercial use.
- `.com` availability check.
- Primary social handle check (IG, X, TikTok).
- **Decision point:** if an identical or near-identical live federal mark exists for related goods/services, the candidate is dead. Kill early, save budget.

### Stage 2 — Full / Comprehensive (3–5 business days)
- USPTO:
  - Phonetic variants (vowel wildcards, consonant swaps, double letters).
  - Truncation/root searching for mark families.
  - Foreign-language equivalents.
  - Related classes (Class 25 clothing often related to Class 14 jewelry, Class 18 leather, Class 35 retail).
  - Design code search if logo in scope.
  - Dead marks within last 5 years (may still have common-law life).
- State:
  - Relevant SOS business entity + state TM databases (focus on states with operations + large-population states).
- Common law:
  - Google/Bing advanced operators (`"mark" -site:uspto.gov`, site-scoped, exact-phrase).
  - Industry trade directories and publications.
  - Yelp / Google Business for local brick-and-mortar use.
- International (if scope requires):
  - WIPO Global Brand Database.
  - EUIPO TMview.
  - CIPO, IP Australia, UK IPO, JPO as needed.
- Domain:
  - Live check on `.com`, `.net`, `.org`, `.io`, `.co`, plus any industry-relevant TLD (`.golf`, `.shop`, `.ai`).
  - WHOIS/RDAP on the `.com` (who owns it, when expires, is it parked/for-sale).
  - Aftermarket listing prices.
- Social:
  - Namechk + KnowEm pass + manual verification on the "big six" (IG, X, TikTok, YouTube, FB, LinkedIn).
  - Note squat-held handles (inactive accounts with the exact name).
- Marketplace:
  - Amazon search (brand + SKU).
  - Etsy shop name search.
  - Shopify (via Google `site:myshopify.com "Mark"`).
  - eBay stores.
- Copyright:
  - Search Copyright Public Catalog for registered slogans/logo art if those elements are in scope.

### Stage 3 — Analysis
- For each hit, record: exact mark, owner, serial/reg #, class(es), status (live/dead), goods/services, first use, and source URL.
- Run DuPont assessment on top hits: mark similarity (sight/sound/meaning), goods/services similarity, channels of trade, sophistication of buyers, strength of senior mark, existence of a family, actual confusion evidence.
- Assess strength of proposed mark on the distinctiveness spectrum.

### Stage 4 — Memo Delivery
- Structured clearance memo (see §5).
- Risk tier (see §6).
- Explicit "not legal advice" disclaimer.
- Attachments: full hit list with screenshots / serial numbers.

## 5. Deliverable Format — Clearance Memo Structure

Based on Lawline CLE materials and Lexis practice-note templates, a complete clearance memo includes:

1. **Header / Engagement scope**
   - Proposed mark (and any stylization/logo).
   - Goods/services description + Nice classes.
   - Territorial scope searched.
   - Date of search, databases covered.
2. **Executive summary**
   - One-paragraph plain-English verdict.
   - Risk tier (green/yellow/red).
3. **Mark strength analysis**
   - Distinctiveness classification (fanciful → generic) with rationale.
   - Registrability implications (e.g., "descriptive — Supplemental Register likely, Principal requires secondary meaning").
4. **Federal conflicts**
   - Table of most-relevant live USPTO marks.
   - Per-hit DuPont summary (similarity of mark + similarity of goods/services at minimum).
5. **State conflicts**
   - State TM and SOS business entity hits that matter.
6. **Common-law conflicts**
   - Web, directory, industry-press, and marketplace findings.
7. **Domain availability**
   - TLD-by-TLD grid; WHOIS/aftermarket notes.
8. **Social handle availability**
   - Platform-by-platform grid.
9. **Marketplace conflicts**
   - Amazon / Etsy / Shopify / eBay hits.
10. **International** (if in scope)
    - WIPO / EU / country-specific findings.
11. **Copyright & design** (if in scope)
    - Slogan/tagline copyright hits; logo design-code conflicts.
12. **Overall risk conclusion**
    - Tier with reasoning.
    - Mitigations (modify mark, narrow classes, limit territory, license, coexistence agreement).
    - Recommended next steps (e.g., attorney review, file ITU application, secure domain, reserve handles).
13. **Limitations & disclaimers**
    - Not legal advice; not an attorney opinion.
    - Search is a snapshot; new filings appear daily.
    - USPTO has ~3–6 month publication lag — some marks may not yet be searchable.
    - Common-law search is inherently non-exhaustive.
    - Recommend licensed trademark counsel before commercial use/filing.
14. **Appendices**
    - Full raw hit lists.
    - Screenshots / exported PDFs of key registrations.
    - Search queries used (for reproducibility).

## 6. Risk Framework (Green / Yellow / Red)

No single formalized industry standard exists, but an aggregated best-practice tiering:

**GREEN — Proceed with standard cautions**
- No identical or highly similar live federal marks in relevant classes.
- No identical common-law use in the same industry.
- Primary `.com` available (or already owned by client).
- Primary social handles available.
- Mark sits at suggestive/arbitrary/fanciful on the spectrum.
- Recommendation: file ITU application, lock domain, lock handles.

**YELLOW — Proceed with caution / modify / attorney review**
- A similar (not identical) live federal mark exists, but in a different class or with meaningfully different goods/services.
- Strong common-law user in a different territory or niche.
- `.com` taken but not actively used, or priced accessibly on aftermarket.
- One or two major social handles taken (squat-style, inactive).
- Mark is suggestive but borderline descriptive.
- Recommendation: attorney opinion required; consider coexistence agreement, narrower class scope, or stylized-mark filing.

**RED — Do not proceed without substantial change**
- Identical or near-identical live federal mark in the same or closely related class.
- Active commercial common-law user in the same industry.
- Mark is generic or clearly descriptive without secondary meaning.
- Dominant domain + handles all owned by an active competing business.
- Known aggressive enforcer (senior user with opposition/litigation history).
- Recommendation: rename. Document the kill rationale so the team doesn't cycle back to the same name.

## 7. Limitations & The Legal Boundary

The specialist is **not a lawyer**. This is non-negotiable. The deliverable must:

- Present **facts and research findings**, not legal conclusions.
- Use risk-flag language ("high risk of §2(d) refusal") rather than outcome language ("this mark will be rejected").
- Include an explicit disclaimer: "This report is a research compilation, not legal advice. A licensed trademark attorney should review before you adopt, file, or invest in this mark."
- Recommend attorney review before any YELLOW or RED finding, and before any filing regardless of tier.
- Never sign, draft, or file a USPTO application on behalf of a client (USPTO UPL rules; LegalZoom has been sued for exactly this kind of boundary violation).
- Never give an opinion on infringement liability, defensive strategy, or litigation posture.

Other inherent limitations to call out in every memo:
- USPTO has a multi-month publication lag; new filings may not yet appear.
- State SOS search UX is inconsistent; some states don't expose fuzzy matching.
- Common-law searches are inherently non-exhaustive (an offline business in another city can hold senior rights).
- Phonetic search is an art — even the best tools miss edge cases.
- Foreign-language equivalents depend on judgment calls about which languages to screen.
- Marketplace searches can't see private Shopify stores or unlisted Amazon SKUs.

## 8. Industry Best Practices

- **Two-stage funnel is standard** — knockout then comprehensive. Don't skip to comprehensive without the knockout filter; don't stop at knockout before brand investment.
- **Search families, not just the mark** — a senior user with MC-, I-, Mc-, etc. family marks casts a wider enforcement shadow than one isolated registration.
- **Prioritize live > dead, but check dead within 5 years** — residual common-law rights can outlive registration.
- **Document the negative** — "no hits in Class X for phonetic variants Y/Z" is evidence if later challenged.
- **Reproducibility** — record every search query so another analyst (or an attorney) can rerun them.
- **Screenshot / PDF-snapshot senior hits** — database records change; the evidence you cite today must still exist in your report next year.
- **Keep a caveat library** — consistent boilerplate disclaimers, refreshed annually against current USPTO UPL guidance.
- **Flag don't conclude** — when in doubt, surface the fact and tier it as YELLOW rather than making a judgment call.
- **AI-assisted, human-verified** — tools like TrademarkNow ExaMatch and TM TKO are force multipliers for phonetic/visual similarity, but every AI-flagged hit must be human-reviewed before inclusion in the memo.
- **Keep a running "kill log"** — names that failed, why, and when. Saves the team from retrying dead-ends.

## 9. Recommended Profile for the AI Team Member

Based on the above, the hire should:

- Operate as a **non-attorney researcher**, explicitly disclaiming legal advice.
- Be fluent in the USPTO Trademark Search system's new query syntax (post-TESS).
- Know the DuPont factors well enough to run a structured similarity analysis, but always frame output as "risk flag," not "legal conclusion."
- Default to the free-tool stack (USPTO, WIPO, TMview, CIPO, Namechk, KnowEm, WHOIS, SOS databases). Call out where a paid tool (CompuMark/Corsearch/TM TKO/Markify) would materially tighten the search, without assuming access.
- Deliver a 14-section clearance memo (see §5) with green/yellow/red tier, plus an attached raw hit list.
- Include standard disclaimers by default, and recommend attorney review on every YELLOW/RED outcome.
- Cognitively: this is an **opus-tier** role. Clearance reasoning requires deep legal context, structured multi-factor analysis, and careful risk calibration — not a checklist-executor.
