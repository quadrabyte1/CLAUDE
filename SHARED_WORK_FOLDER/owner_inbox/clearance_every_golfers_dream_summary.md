## Risk tier: YELLOW

Not GREEN — the mark is **laudatory/borderline descriptive under §2(e)(1)**, the primary **.com is not available**, and there are meaningful common-law neighbors. Not RED — no identical live federal registration, no US common-law blocker in the plaque category, core social handles partially free.

## Top 3 headline findings

1. **Mark strength is the real problem, not a competitor.** The USPTO treats laudatory phrases ("best," "greatest," "supreme," "preferred") as merely descriptive. "Every Golfer's Dream" is structurally identical to that pattern. Expect a §2(e)(1) office action; realistic path is **Supplemental Register** (not Principal) unless Thomas proves acquired distinctiveness after years of use. Even if registered, scope is narrow and expensive to police.
2. **Common-law shadows in golf.** (a) **Golfer's Dream Golf Club** — active Canadian golf course in Port Perry, Ontario, ~16+ years operating, **golfersdream.ca**; relevant if Thomas ever files Madrid into Canada or expands into Class 41. (b) **"A Golfer's Dream"** — 2007 book by Larry Berle, Minnesota Book Award nominee, still sold on Amazon; relevant if Thomas expands into golf media/travel content. (c) Crowded "Dream Golf" / "Dream [Golf Noun]" field (Dream Golf Pro Shop IL, Dream Golf Resorts, Dream Golf Shop).
3. **The phrase is in heavy descriptive circulation.** YouTube, Instagram, PGA captions, Golf Digest-adjacent blogs use "every golfer's dream" constantly as laudatory copy ("Brad Faxon's office is every golfer's dream," "this Airbnb is every golfer's dream," etc.). That noise lowers the mark's distinctiveness AND makes organic discovery of Thomas's brand harder.

## Mark-strength verdict

**Weak / suggestive-but-leaning-descriptive.** Laudatory construction of the form "the [idealized buyer]'s dream" almost always gets a §2(e)(1) refusal on the Principal Register. Expect Supplemental Register as the realistic first home for this mark. If Thomas wants a brand asset with real enforcement muscle, a more distinctive name will repay the effort. If Thomas wants this specific name for emotional/marketing reasons, the name can still be used in commerce — just with realistic expectations about what the trademark will and won't protect.
